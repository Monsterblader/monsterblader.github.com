December 29, 2012
I love underscore.js. I was introduced to it as an exercise for Catalyst. It was confusing, at first. While I have some programming background, the concept of passing functions as parameters is new to me.

The exercise was to write each of underscore's functions from scratch. Writing each function was pretty easy; none of them requred sophisticated ooncepts. The thinking became difficult when we started using the functions to define other functions. I had a really hard time following the parameters as they flowed through the callbacks.

The main confusion for me came in dealing with the parameters that went with the callback functions. I was confused about how an anonymous function that was defined before it was called could receive parameters. I was also confused about how to pass parameters, as well.

I and my partner maanaged to get through a good number of the assigned functions. My understanding was pretty shaky, but all of our functions passed the included Jasmine tests, so we felt good about what we accomplished.

What changed foor me was my willingness to use underscore everywhere that I could, as Shawn, one of the instructors, suggested. Figuring out where and when to use functions was pretty easy. Figuring out how to use the callbacks was still pretty challenging. I was still having problems knowing what variables to use.

I think that, at that point, I just went on faith that every function would pass value, key, and array to the callback no matter what. After that, I was able plan my callbacks, depend on certain information to be available to me, and pass additional parameters as needed. I still wasn't fully visualizing how the functions and callbacks were interacting, but my code was running.

December 15, 2012
I'm currently taking a class in San Francisco, which is teaching HTML, CSS, and Javascript, among other things. It's an intensive program, running from 9:00 A.M. to 8:00 P.M. The teaching style is more on the order of 'here is the elephant. Now go and eat it,' than the spoon-feeding, grain-of-rice style that most schools endorse.

Something that's been on my mind since early in the course is learning styles and if people are even aware of the concept, much less their own. I think that it's largely accepted that there's one teaching model - that of the teacher talking at the class for x amount of time.

Consequently, I think that most people are unaware that there are different styles of learning. I didn't think about this sort of thing when I was younger, also that's how it was so I didn't have reason to consider it. Now that I am in this class, I think about it a lot, for two primary reasons. The first is, because the rate of exposure is so high, I dont have a lot of time to assimilate material and even less time to review it.

The second reason is because the course is so new and flexible, there is a great deal of willingness to reconsider how things are done.

I thought that I had a pretty good idea of my learning style, but I seem to have gained quite a bit of nuance to my understanding. I was already aware that I preferred to get a few general pieces, play with that foundation, hit a wall, look up a few things, then push through.
